Fitting & Generalization

The most fundamental question in all of ML: does your model actually work on data it hasn’t seen? A model that memorizes the training set is worthless in production. This page is the classic material — over/underfitting, bias-variance, regularization — and it applies at every rung of the ladder, from a GBM to a fine-tuned LLM.

Where this sits on the ladder
Rung-agnostic — it governs every model you train. Whether you fit gradient-boosted trees or fine-tune a transformer, the same discipline decides success: hold out data honestly, watch the generalization gap, and regularize. Overfitting is the failure mode that quietly makes a great-looking model useless in production.

Overfitting vs underfitting

  • Underfitting — the model is too simple to capture the pattern. High error on both train and test. (A straight line through a curve.)
  • Overfitting — the model memorizes the training data, including its noise. Low train error, high test error. (A wiggly curve through every point.)
  • Good fit — captures the real signal, ignores the noise. Low error on both, with a small gap between them.
Same points, three models. The overfit curve nails every training point — including the noise — and will generalize worst. The good fit ignores the wiggles.

The bias-variance tradeoff

The lens behind fitting. Total error decomposes into:

  • Bias — error from wrong assumptions / too-simple a model (→ underfitting).
  • Variance — error from sensitivity to the particular training set (→ overfitting).
  • Irreducible noise — the floor you can’t beat.

Classically, lowering one raises the other, and you seek the sweet spot. Think of a dartboard:

Variance = scatter (inconsistent, overfit); bias = a consistent miss off the bullseye (too-simple, underfit). You want tight and centered.

Train / validation / test

The discipline that keeps you honest. Split your data three ways:

  • Train — fit the model.
  • Validation — tune hyperparameters & pick models. You look at this a lot.
  • Test — touch once, at the end, to estimate real-world performance.

With limited data, use k-fold cross-validation: rotate which fold is held out and average, for a more stable estimate. The generalization gap (train vs validation performance) is your overfitting gauge.

The cardinal sin
If you tune against the test set, it stops being an honest estimate — you’ve overfit to it through your own choices. Look at test once. For time-series, split by time, never randomly, or you leak the future into the past.

Data leakage — the silent killer

Data leakage is when information that won’t be available at prediction time sneaks into training — giving amazing offline scores that collapse in production. It’s the most common reason a model that “worked” fails on launch. Classic forms:

  • Target leakage — a feature that’s a proxy for (or derived from) the label (e.g. “was_refunded” to predict “will_churn”).
  • Train/test contamination — scaling/encoding fit on the full dataset before splitting, or duplicate rows across splits.
  • Temporal leakage — using future information to predict the past.
FDE instinct
When results look too good, suspect leakage before celebrating. Ask: “Would this feature actually exist, with this value, at the moment we predict?” If not, it’s leaking.

Double descent (modern note)

The classic bias-variance U-curve says error rises once a model gets too big. Modern deep learning complicated that: as you keep growing an over-parameterized model past the interpolation point, test error can drop again — “double descent.” It’s why enormous LLMs generalize despite having far more parameters than data points. The classic intuition still guides day-to-day work, but know that “bigger always overfits” isn’t strictly true at modern scale.

Interview Q&A

coreHow do you detect and fix overfitting?

Detect it by the gap: low training error but much higher validation/test error means the model memorized the training set. Fix it by reducing capacity or adding regularization — L2/weight decay, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation — and, best of all, more data. For trees, cap depth / increase min-samples; for nets, dropout + early stopping. Underfitting (high error everywhere) is the opposite fix: a more expressive model or better features.

coreExplain the bias-variance tradeoff.

Expected error decomposes into bias (error from an overly simple model — underfitting), variance (sensitivity to the specific training sample — overfitting), and irreducible noise. Simple models have high bias/low variance; complex ones low bias/high variance. Classically you balance them for minimum total error. Regularization trades a little bias for a lot less variance, which is usually a good deal.

trapYour model scores 99% offline but fails in production. First thing you suspect?

Data leakage. A feature that won’t exist (or won’t have that value) at prediction time — target leakage, temporal leakage, or fitting preprocessing on the whole dataset before splitting — inflates offline scores and vanishes in production. I’d audit every feature with “is this available, with this value, at inference time?”, check that scalers/encoders were fit on train only, and verify no row overlap or future information across splits.

seniorWhy can’t you use the test set to pick your model?

Because every decision you make based on the test set fits your process to that set, so it stops being an unbiased estimate of real-world performance — you’ve leaked it through model selection. That’s why you tune on validation and touch test once at the end. With limited data, k-fold cross-validation gives a more stable estimate without burning a separate test set prematurely.

seniorDoes “bigger model = more overfitting” always hold?

Not at modern scale. The classic U-curve says test error rises once capacity is too high, but deep learning shows double descent: past the interpolation threshold, hugely over-parameterized models can generalize better again, aided by implicit regularization from the optimizer and by scale. It’s a big reason massive LLMs work. The classic tradeoff still guides most practical modeling, but it isn’t a universal law at the extremes.

trapHow do you split a time-series dataset?

Chronologically — train on the past, validate/test on the future, never a random shuffle. A random split lets the model see future points while predicting earlier ones, which leaks information and gives optimistic scores that won’t hold live. Use forward-chaining / rolling-window cross-validation so every evaluation only uses data that would have been available at that time.

Code: spotting overfitting with early stopping

The most useful diagnostic in ML: plot train vs validation loss. When validation stops improving while train keeps dropping, you’re overfitting — and early stopping halts you at the sweet spot.

pythontrain vs validation loss + early stopping (PyTorch)
# The generalization gap in code: watch train vs validation loss, stop early.
import torch, torch.nn as nn

# Assume X_tr, y_tr, X_val, y_val are float tensors already prepared.
model = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(20, 128), nn.ReLU(),
                      nn.Dropout(0.3),            # dropout = regularization
                      nn.Linear(128, 1))
loss_fn = nn.BCEWithLogitsLoss()
# weight_decay = L2 regularization, another overfitting defense.
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4)

best_val = float("inf")
patience, wait = 5, 0                              # early-stopping patience

for epoch in range(200):
    # --- train step ---
    model.train()
    opt.zero_grad()
    tr_loss = loss_fn(model(X_tr), y_tr)
    tr_loss.backward()
    opt.step()

    # --- validation check (no gradients) ---
    model.eval()
    with torch.no_grad():
        val_loss = loss_fn(model(X_val), y_val).item()

    # The gap between tr_loss and val_loss IS your overfitting signal:
    # train keeps dropping while val flattens/rises -> overfitting.
    if val_loss < best_val - 1e-4:
        best_val, wait = val_loss, 0
        torch.save(model.state_dict(), "best.pt")  # keep the best-generalizing model
    else:
        wait += 1
        if wait >= patience:                       # val stopped improving -> stop
            print(f"Early stop at epoch {epoch}, best val {best_val:.4f}")
            break

# Load the checkpoint from BEFORE it started overfitting.
model.load_state_dict(torch.load("best.pt"))
The habit to build
Always plot train vs validation together. One curve tells you nothing; the gap between them is the single most informative picture in applied ML — it tells you to add capacity, add regularization, or stop.
